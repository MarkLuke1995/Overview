Machine Elements

The Machine Elements project was a semester long project that incorporated two classes worth of material, Machine Elements I and II. The idea of the project was to create an exhibit for children that showed some mechanical or physics phenomenon. Our idea was to teach kids about projectile motion using a simple ball launcher. The ball launcher consisted of a spring, ball bearing, wire rope, worm gear and regular gear. Kids would pll back on the wire rope after rotating the launcher with the worm gear and ball bearing to aim at different targets within the exhibit. The design itself had to maintain a minimum safety factor of 1.25 for each component while being used for an entire year.

I was in charge of designing the spring for the machine. Initially, I attempted to design the spring from the top down, determining the geometric constraints and force needed to launch an baseball about 10 feet. Ignoring friction losses, I came up with a spring constant to design my spring around. However, using this constant and required safety factor, I calculated a long spring with an extremely small wire diameter. The spring would not exist anywhere. So, I decided to take a bottom up approach and find several springs that roughly fit the geometric dimensions needed. I found how much these needed to be deflected to launch the ball the required distance, and then found the stresses experienced by the spring. Analyzing for fatigue stress, one spring was found to maintain the needed safety factor of 1.25, and this was chosen.

Injection Mold

The injection molding project consisted of designing a logo or design in SolidWORKS conforming to several design criteria (no sharp corners, minimum widths or fillets of 0.125in, flat surfaces at 0.5 or 0.25in depth for ejector pin holes). These criteria were primarily there for manufacturing purposes, as the smallest tool diameter we were using was 0.125in, along with sharp corners being impossible to mill out. Then, using SolidWORKS mold tools, a mold cavity of the design was created. To CNC the cavity, HSMWorks CAM was used to create different functions to mill it out. I used an adaptive and pocket clear to mill out the majority of the work, and contour as a finishing operation.

MagLev Controller Design

My MagLev Controller Design project was another semester long project for my Systems Integration and Controls class. The idea of the project was to design a controller for a simplified magnetic levitation system in one direction. The controller had to perform within a specific set of parameters for a height step reference and force step input. Our final controller design ended up being a lead-lead-lag, and was just outside of the control panels for the step reference. Unfortunately, the force step input cause the system to become unstable. This was the best design we were able to find, and shows the importance of trying many different controller designs and gains to obtain control parameters.

Going a little more in-depth into each part of the three project parts, the first part entailed determining the static equation of motion (EoM) for the train in the "y" (vertical) direction and linearizing it around a operating point. This showed that the only forces on the train were its weight and the supporting magnetic force. Magnitude and phase plots were drawn from this EoM. A simple block diagram of the system was made in simulink and simulated two force step inputs, one without a reference signal and one with. The second part added in the element of coils to use the electromagnetic force to control the height of the train. The addition of the coils added a new EoM due to the coil acting like an inductor. This was included into the simulink diagram and the new response was simulated, showing the system is unstable without control input. The final part of the project was designing a controller for the dynamic system found. Control parameters were given, and system damping ratio and natural frequency were found from these. Then, a transfer function of the system was determined. Using MatLab's Sisotool, it was shown that P-control was unable to make the system stable. So, a lead-lead-lag controller was chosen to attempt to get the system to conform to the control parameters. Our final design was able to control the system with a step reference but was unable to for a disturbance input. Reports are available for download by clicking the button below.

Lab of Sound and Vibration Research

This section will outline the research I performed with Dr. Harne in the Lab of Sound and Vibration Research. I was considered a SIMCenter intern and worked on his origami acoustics project. The goal of the research is to come up with ways to control sound pressure at specific points in space without having to do it solely computationally with phase shifts. By doing this with origami arrays, the transducers could become much smaller, easier to transport and much less computationally costly. After reading several of his papers (Origami Acoustics, Adaptive Acoustic Energy Delivery ) and learning how to use InkScape and Rigid Origami Simulator, I was given an inhouse built FEA code in MatLab for determining sound pressure levels (SPL) at points in space above a specific section of the arrays. This code was limited to arrays that were strictly rectangular, as three of the corners needed to be detected to rotate the array back to its original plane (Rigid Origami Simulator rotated the arrays as they folded up). Using this knowledge and a muira-ori array (seen right) I created a function to determine the farthest three points from the origin that all stayed on the same plane. These points were rotated back to the original plane before the array was broken up into its elements and analyzed for sound pressure. The pictures on the right show the array I was working on before leaving the lab in January 2018.

One of the biggest things I learned by doing this research was the importance of verifying a model. When I initially was toying with the FEA code given to me, I was blindly throwing it muira-ori arrays and taking the results as truth. This was a poor way of going about it, as I really had no way of knowing if the results showed the correct SPL. Dr. Harne guided me to a solution to verify that my model was working. I created an arc of an array at a specific length, and input a sound frequency that would be equal to the arc length. By doing this, I would expect that the SPL would reach a local maximum at a radius length away from the center of the array when it is folded up to create a half circle. When I did this, the code I had initially did not indicate these results, and I knew something was wrong with my model. I narrowed it down to the analysis portion of the code. After consulting with Dr. Harne, he sent me a different analysis technique to use. Inputting my test case to verify the model gave the results I expected, a peak in SPL near the radius above the center of the array when folded in half. After this point, I was able to start exploring other arrays, and attempted to create a spherical muira-ori array.

Fundamentals of Engineering Honors: Robot

The Fundamentals of Engineering Honors (FEH) Robot project was an entire course my second semester of first year. With a team of three others, the goal of the project was to create an autonomous robot using the FEH Proteus (microcontroller) that completes a set of tasks on a course, all while remaining under budget and using limited resources available to us from the class store. The theme our year was an artic research outpost, and the robot had to navigate to several areas of the course to grab a sandbag, turn a crank, press buttons in a specific order, drop off the sandbag in a garage, and finally flip a switch. I was in charge of the fabrication of the main structural pieces of the robot and coding of the microcontroller. The main frame was made of wood, and I machined that out using a bandsaw and belt sander. The crank mechanism and button pusher were created in CAD models and 3D printed before being assembled to the robot. Coding the proteus was in C and required me to be in many of the open labs to test out code after uploading it. The hardest part of the coding was controlling the motors to determine distance moved. In competition, our robot ended up not performing optimally; however, our group still did fine in the class.
